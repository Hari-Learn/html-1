RIP in Routing (Routing Information Protocol)

RIP (Routing Information Protocol) is one of the oldest dynamic routing protocols used in computer networks. It works based on the distance-vector routing algorithm.

How RIP Works

RIP uses hop count as its metric to determine the best path to a destination. The maximum hop count allowed is 15. Any route with 16 hops is considered unreachable.

Internal or External Use?

RIP is mainly designed for internal routing within an organization. It is used as an Interior Gateway Protocol (IGP).

  • Internal Networks: ✅ Yes, RIP can be used
  • Between Organizations (Internet): ❌ Not suitable

Why RIP is NOT Used Externally

  • Limited to 15 hops
  • Slow convergence
  • Periodic updates every 30 seconds
  • Not scalable for large networks

Where RIP is Still Used

  • Small networks
  • Lab environments
  • Educational purposes
  • Simple internal routing setups

Conclusion

RIP is considered a legacy routing protocol today. While it is simple and easy to configure, modern networks prefer protocols like OSPF or EIGRP due to better performance and scalability.
