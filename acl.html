ACL (Access Control List) in Routers

Zero → Intermediate | Explain Like I’m 10

1️⃣ The Problem (Start Here)

Imagine your home has no doors, no locks. Anyone can walk in, go to your room, open your fridge, or break things.

A network without rules is the same. Any computer can:

  • Access private servers
  • Steal data
  • Send harmful traffic

2️⃣ Why Is This a Big Problem?

Routers connect many networks together (like roads). If there are no traffic rules:

  • Hackers can enter easily
  • Employees can access forbidden sites
  • Servers can be attacked

❌ No control = No security

3️⃣ What Is ACL?

ACL (Access Control List) is a list of rules that tells the router:

"Who is allowed and who is NOT allowed to pass."

Think of ACL like a security guard checking:

  • Who you are
  • Where you are going
  • What you are trying to do

4️⃣ How ACL Solves the Problem

ACL works using two simple actions:

✅ PERMIT
You are allowed to pass
❌ DENY
You are blocked

Every packet (data) is checked against ACL rules one by one.

5️⃣ Basic ACL Concepts (Very Important)

  • ACL is applied on a router interface
  • Rules are checked top to bottom
  • First match = decision made
  • There is an invisible rule at the end:

❌ "Deny everything else"

6️⃣ Types of ACL

Standard ACL

Checks only:

  • Source IP address

Simple but less powerful

Extended ACL

Checks:

  • Source IP
  • Destination IP
  • Protocol (HTTP, FTP, etc.)
  • Port numbers

More control, more secure

7️⃣ Real-Life Example (ELI10)

School rule:

"Students can enter classrooms, but NOT the staff room."

ACL version:

  • Permit students → classroom
  • Deny students → staff room

8️⃣ Intermediate Understanding

ACLs can be applied in two directions:

  • Inbound – traffic entering router
  • Outbound – traffic leaving router

Best practice:

✔ Place extended ACLs close to the source

✅ Final Summary

  • Networks need control
  • ACL is a rule list in routers
  • It permits or denies traffic
  • Standard = simple
  • Extended = powerful
